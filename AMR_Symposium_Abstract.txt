AI-Driven Surveillance for Antimicrobial-Resistant Typhoid Fever: 
Integrating Epidemic Modeling with Machine Learning for Resource-Limited Settings in Sub-Saharan Africa

Sergius Justus Chesami Nyah
University of Buea, Cameroon
sergius.justus@ubuea.cm

================================================================================
ABSTRACT (300 words max)
================================================================================

Antimicrobial-resistant typhoid fever poses an escalating public health threat in Sub-Saharan Africa, where surveillance infrastructure remains limited. Traditional resistance monitoring requires expensive laboratory equipment unavailable in most African clinical settings, creating dangerous delays in detecting resistance emergence and enabling unchecked spread of drug-resistant Salmonella Typhi strains.

We developed an integrated AI system combining epidemic modeling with machine learning for real-time AMR surveillance. The framework consists of two components: (1) An extended SIRS compartmental model tracking sensitive and resistant typhoid strains, incorporating antibiotic treatment rates and resistance development dynamics, (2) A lightweight convolutional neural network classifier predicting resistance patterns from readily available clinical data and basic microscopy images.

Our approach prioritizes computational efficiency to enable deployment on standard clinic computers. The CNN architecture uses only 21.7M parameters (96% smaller than typical models) while maintaining high accuracy. The epidemic model incorporates African-specific parameters: 80% treatment rate, 15% resistance emergence during therapy, 14-day illness duration.

Simulations over 2-year periods demonstrate the system accurately tracks resistance emergence from initial 9% to 65% of cases. Peak infections occur at day 180-210 with resistance levels reaching 45-55%. The ML classifier achieves 88% accuracy in predicting resistance from clinical presentations, with inference time under 2 seconds on CPU-only hardware.

This system enables continuous AMR surveillance in resource-constrained settings without requiring expensive diagnostics. Early resistance detection allows targeted antibiotic stewardship, potentially reducing inappropriate fluoroquinolone use by 30-40%. The approach is generalizable to other bacterial infections endemic to Africa, addressing a critical gap in global health security.

Keywords: Antimicrobial Resistance, Typhoid Fever, Epidemic Modeling, Machine Learning, Sub-Saharan Africa, Point-of-Care Diagnostics

HOW DOES YOUR WORK RELATE TO APPLICATION OF AI IN AMR?
================================================================================

Our work applies AI to AMR surveillance through two integrated approaches. First, we use machine learning (convolutional neural networks) to predict antibiotic resistance directly from clinical data that is routinely available in African healthcare settings: patient symptoms, treatment history, demographics, and basic microscopy images. This replaces the need for expensive molecular diagnostics (PCR, whole-genome sequencing) or advanced culture systems (MALDI-TOF) that are unavailable in most Sub-Saharan African clinics.

Second, we employ computational epidemic modeling to track resistance dynamics at the population level. The SIRS model simulates how resistant strains emerge and spread under different treatment scenarios, helping policymakers understand the impact of antibiotic stewardship interventions before implementation.

The key innovation is computational efficiency: our AI models are deliberately lightweight (21.7M parameters vs 500M+ in typical deep learning systems) to run on standard clinic computers without GPU acceleration. This democratizes AMR surveillance technology for resource-limited settings. By predicting resistance in under 2 seconds, clinicians can make real-time treatment decisions, reducing empirical use of last-line antibiotics and slowing resistance spread.

Our approach transforms AMR surveillance from a centralized laboratory function requiring expensive infrastructure into a distributed point-of-care capability accessible to frontline African healthcare workers.

INNOVATIVE CONTRIBUTION TO POLICY, PRACTICE, AND/OR RESEARCH (200 words max)
================================================================================

POLICY IMPACT: Our system provides actionable data for national antibiotic stewardship policies in African countries. Real-time resistance tracking enables evidence-based updates to empirical treatment guidelines, helping governments phase out failing antibiotics (e.g., chloramphenicol) and preserve last-line agents (fluoroquinolones, azithromycin).

PRACTICE TRANSFORMATION: This is the first AMR surveillance tool designed specifically for resource-constrained African clinics. By running on standard computers without internet connectivity, it overcomes the two major barriers to surveillance adoption: cost and infrastructure requirements. Clinicians receive immediate resistance predictions rather than waiting weeks for reference laboratory results.

RESEARCH INNOVATION: We demonstrate that epidemic modeling can be tightly coupled with machine learning for AMR surveillance. Most existing approaches use either modeling or ML, but not both. Our integrated framework allows predictions at both individual (patient-level resistance) and population scales (outbreak dynamics), providing insights unavailable from either method alone.

SCALABILITY: The lightweight architecture enables deployment across hundreds of clinics simultaneously, creating a decentralized surveillance network. This addresses WHO's call for enhanced AMR monitoring in low-income countries while respecting limited computational resources.

Our approach is immediately applicable to other priority pathogens (E. coli, Klebsiella, Staphylococcus aureus) endemic to Africa.
